What the Exam Tests

Part I – General Language Proficiency (Items 1–75)

  • Sentence Completion (1–9): Choose the word that best finishes a sentence.
  • Synonyms in Context (10–17): Find the closest meaning to an underlined word.
  • Synonyms (18–38): Match a word to its closest synonym.
  • Antonyms (39–50): Select the word opposite in meaning.
  • Idioms (51–75): Identify the meaning of an underlined idiomatic expression.

Part II – Court Terms, Usage & Ethics (Items 76–135)

  • Court Sentence Completion (76–111): Court-specific vocabulary in context.
  • Court-Related Questions (112–121): Knowledge of court procedures.
  • Sequence (122–125): Correct order of events in court proceedings.
  • Professional Conduct Questions (126–127): Ethical responsibilities.
  • Scenarios (128–135): Ethical dilemmas an interpreter may face.

Important Rules

  • Arrive early — late arrivals are not admitted, no exceptions.
  • No phones, dictionaries, notes, pens, water, watches, or bags in the testing room. Pencils are provided.
  • Leaving the room does not pause your exam clock.
  • Passing the written exam does not make you a certified interpreter — it is one of several requirements.
  • If you fail, wait and study before retaking. Most states allow no more than twice per calendar year.

✏ Study Tips & Resources

📖 English Language Proficiency (Part I)
  • Read widely: major newspapers, American literature, professional journals. Active reading is the best vocabulary builder.
  • Review antonyms, synonyms, and idioms systematically — use flashcard apps like Anki.
  • Study TOEFL preparation materials; the vocabulary difficulty is comparable.
  • Review the Federal Court Interpreter Certification Examination (FCICE) Phase One study guides.
  • Use the Idioms panel in this guide to review all idioms before your exam.
🏛 Court Terminology (Part II)
  • Read legal news in major newspapers; look up every unfamiliar term in Black's Law Dictionary.
  • Visit your local courthouse and observe actual proceedings (criminal, civil, and family).
  • Take a criminal justice or court administration course at a college or university.
  • Study the standard stages of a criminal case: Arrest → Arraignment → Trial → Sentencing.
  • Review glossaries published by the U.S. federal courts online.
⚖ Ethics & Professional Conduct (Part II)
  • Study Utah's Code of Professional Responsibility for Court Interpreters.
  • Join NAJIT or ATA and review their codes of ethics.
  • Core principle: interpreters convey meaning accurately and impartially — they do not explain, advise, summarize, or editorialize.
  • When in doubt about a scenario: the interpreter's role is to be the voice of another person, not an advocate.
🧠 Test-Taking Strategy
  • 2h 15m for 135 questions — roughly 1 minute per question. Don't linger.
  • For vocabulary/synonyms: eliminate obvious wrong answers first.
  • For idioms: think everyday speech meaning, not literal meaning.
  • For ethics scenarios: always choose the option that preserves impartiality and accuracy first.
  • For sequences: Arrest → Initial Appearance → Bail Hearing → Arraignment → Preliminary Hearing → Grand Jury → Trial → Sentencing.
